Historical Background

The Ellora Caves, located in Maharashtra, India, are a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most remarkable examples of ancient rock-cut architecture. Carved between the 6th and 10th centuries, the site features 34 caves that were excavated out of the Charanandri hills. These caves are a unique fusion of three major religions—Buddhism, Hinduism, and Jainism—reflecting the religious harmony of ancient India. The most famous structure at Ellora is the Kailasa Temple (Cave 16), a massive monolithic temple dedicated to Lord Shiva, which was carved from a single rock and showcases incredible craftsmanship and artistic skill. The Ellora Caves remain a testament to India's rich cultural heritage and the creativity of its ancient civilizations.

Visitor Rules

  • Avoid touching or climbing on the carvings and sculptures.
  • Do not write, scratch, or deface the walls or structures.
  • Photography is allowed, but avoid flash inside caves and respect any signs that prohibit photography.
  • Maintain silence, especially in inner sanctums and meditation areas.

Safety Precautions

  • Protect yourself from the sun with hats, sunscreen, and water.
  • Oils from hands can damage ancient carvings.
  • Carry basic medicines if you're sensitive to dust or have allergies.
  • Avoid carrying heavy bags as the terrain involves a lot of movement.
